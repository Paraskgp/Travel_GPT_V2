# TravelGPT MLP PR FAQ

## Press Release

**Introducing TravelGPT: The Discovery Engine for Trips Worth Remembering**

Seattle, WA - Today we are introducing **TravelGPT**, a destination discovery engine that helps travelers quickly understand the best, most distinctive, and most relevant things to do in any place without spending weeks watching videos, reading blogs, comparing duplicate tour listings, and stitching together scattered recommendations.

Travel planning often begins with excitement and quickly turns into work. A traveler searches "things to do in Maui" or "best experiences in Paris" and is immediately buried under hundreds of results: repeated tour listings, generic top-10 lists, outdated blog posts, sponsored roundups, and conflicting opinions. The traveler does not just need more recommendations. They need a clean, de-duplicated, trustworthy view of what is actually worth considering.

TravelGPT solves that first and most painful step: **figuring out what matters at a destination**.

Users enter a city, region, island, national park, or landmark area, and TravelGPT returns a curated set of experience cards organized by useful trip-planning categories: most visited, unique local experiences, food and drink, adventure, nature, hiking, culture, family-friendly, date night, rainy day, day trips, and more. Each card explains what the experience is, why it is worth doing, who it is best for, how much effort it takes, when to go, what to watch out for, and where to learn or book more.

Unlike booking sites that show dozens of near-identical "manta ray night snorkel" listings from different operators, TravelGPT clusters duplicates into a single clean experience. Instead of forcing users to manually compare hundreds of versions of the same activity, TravelGPT helps them understand the underlying opportunity: what the experience is, why it is special, what the main variants are, and how to choose between them.

TravelGPT also adapts to personal preferences without prematurely hiding the destination. A vegetarian traveler can see food experiences ranked and filtered appropriately, with incompatible options flagged and explained. A family with young children can prioritize kid-friendly activities. A hiker can see trails, viewpoints, and day trips rise to the top. A culture-focused traveler can lean into museums, workshops, neighborhoods, and local rituals. The product helps users down-select quickly while still preserving the joy of discovery.

Food is treated as a first-class way to experience a place, not as a secondary restaurant search. In India, a traveler may want a street-food crawl, regional thali, biryani trail, tea estate visit, spice market, or cooking class. In Edinburgh or Kentucky, whisky may be one of the defining reasons to travel. In Mexico City, Tokyo, Naples, New Orleans, or Bangkok, the food map is inseparable from the travel map. TravelGPT helps users understand the dishes, neighborhoods, producers, markets, crawls, tours, and food rituals that make a destination memorable.

TravelGPT also looks for the local nuance that makes an experience work. A beach recommendation should not just say "go to this beach." It should explain the best time of day, parking difficulty, whether afternoon heat is a problem, whether sunset is the reason to go, what to bring, and what nearby activity makes the stop better. A viewpoint may be ordinary at noon and magical at sunrise. A lake visit may become far better if the traveler knows to rent bikes. A famous attraction may be worth doing only with a timed entry, a side entrance, or a nearby food stop. TravelGPT should reduce the traveler's fear of missing out by surfacing these practical hacks before the trip, not after the user has already arrived.

The first version of TravelGPT is not a full-service travel agency and does not handle payments, confirmations, flights, or hotel bookings. It is intentionally focused on the job travelers need before any itinerary or booking can be trusted: **discovering the right set of things they could do**.

In the next phase, TravelGPT will turn a user's selected experiences into a realistic itinerary with timing, geography, opening hours, travel links, and booking links. But the first promise is simpler and more important:

**Type a destination. Understand what is worth doing there. Stop wondering what you missed.**

TravelGPT brings calm, taste, and structure to the messiest part of travel planning.

## Customer Problem

Travelers do not suffer from a lack of information. They suffer from too much unstructured, repetitive, low-confidence information.

Before a trip, a motivated traveler often checks:

- Google Search
- Google Maps
- YouTube travel videos
- TikTok and Instagram reels
- TripAdvisor
- Reddit threads
- Viator, GetYourGuide, Klook, Airbnb Experiences, and other booking sites
- Blogs, local event calendars, and hotel concierge pages

Each source answers part of the question, but none of them gives the traveler a clean mental model of the destination. The user still has to ask:

- What are the genuinely important things to know about this place?
- Which recommendations are duplicates under different names?
- Which experiences are tourist traps, and which are actually worthwhile?
- Which activities are famous but skippable for my situation?
- Which hidden or local experiences would I never find on my own?
- Which foods, drinks, markets, crawls, producers, or culinary neighborhoods define this place?
- What local timing, packing, routing, or crowd-avoidance hack would make this experience better?
- What is good for kids, vegetarians, hikers, food lovers, or older parents?
- Which options are seasonal, weather-dependent, or hard to book?
- What are the day trips or nearby places I should consider?

The result is anxiety. Travelers keep researching because they are afraid of missing something. They do not know when they are "done."

TravelGPT gives them that stopping point.

## Product Promise

TravelGPT is the fastest way to understand what is worth doing at a destination.

It helps users:

- See the major experience categories for a destination
- Discover both famous and lesser-known options
- Treat food and drink as a core travel experience, including crawls, markets, iconic dishes, producers, tastings, classes, and food neighborhoods
- Avoid wading through duplicate tour listings
- Understand why each experience matters
- Learn the local timing, packing, routing, crowd, and weather nuances that make an experience succeed
- Compare experiences by effort, audience, timing, seasonality, and booking difficulty
- Apply light personal preferences to improve ranking and relevance
- Save a shortlist of experiences for a future itinerary

The product should feel like a well-traveled friend who has already done the research, removed the noise, and handed the user a beautiful, organized board of possibilities.

Priya and Arjun are planning a trip to the Big Island of Hawaii. They are excited, but within one evening they have opened 30 tabs. Every site mentions manta ray night snorkeling, volcanoes, coffee farms, beaches, stargazing, waterfalls, and snorkeling spots, but the recommendations blur together.

On booking sites, they find dozens of manta ray tours that look almost identical. Some leave from different harbors. Some are better for strong swimmers. Some are more family-friendly. Some include wetsuits. Some have better cancellation policies. They are not sure whether they are choosing an experience or just choosing an operator.

YouTube videos add more places to the list. Blogs contradict each other. Google Maps reviews are helpful but scattered. Reddit has strong opinions but no structure. After several nights of research, Priya and Arjun still do not feel confident. They have more information, but not more clarity.

### After TravelGPT

Priya opens TravelGPT and enters "Big Island, Hawaii."

TravelGPT returns a destination board organized into clear categories:

- Signature experiences
- Adventure
- Nature and scenic drives
- Beaches and snorkeling
- Food and farms
- Culture and history
- Local tips and timing
- Kid-friendly
- Rainy day options
- Day trips and nearby clusters

The manta ray night snorkel appears once as a clean experience card, not as 45 competing listings. The card explains what the experience is, why it is special, where it usually departs from, who it is good for, common variants, booking considerations, and what can go wrong.

The food layer is just as explicit. Instead of showing random restaurant lists, TravelGPT explains which foods and farms define the island, where coffee farms fit into the trip, which local dishes are worth seeking out, which stops work for vegetarians, and which food experiences are worth planning around rather than treating as an afterthought.

The cards also include local intelligence: when to go for the best light, when to avoid crowds, what to pack, whether the afternoon sun changes the experience, and what nearby add-on makes the stop feel complete.

Priya adds preferences: vegetarian food, moderate adventure, no scuba, interested in coffee farms, traveling with parents, prefers low-stress mornings. TravelGPT re-ranks the board. Some options move up. A few are flagged as less suitable with short explanations. Nothing feels like a black box.

Within 20 minutes, Priya and Arjun have a shortlist of 12 experiences they are excited about. They no longer feel like they need to watch every video on the internet. They understand the destination well enough to make choices.

That is the first lovable moment.

## Minimum Lovable Product

The MLP focuses on **destination experience discovery and down-selection**.

### User Input

The user can enter:

- Destination: city, region, island, national park, neighborhood, or landmark area
- Optional trip context: dates or season, trip length, party type, rough budget, interests, dietary needs, mobility needs, pace, and things to avoid

The product must work even if the user only enters a destination. Personalization improves ranking, but it should not be required to get value.

### Destination Board

For each destination, TravelGPT generates a structured board of experience categories. Initial categories should include:

- Most visited
- Unique or local experiences
- Food and drink
- Food crawls, markets, and culinary neighborhoods
- Adventure
- Nature
- Hiking and outdoors
- Culture and history
- Arts and workshops
- Family-friendly
- Romantic or special occasion
- Rainy day
- Nightlife
- Shopping and markets
- Day trips
- Seasonal or time-bound

Categories should adapt to the destination. A national park should emphasize trails, viewpoints, scenic drives, permits, wildlife, weather, and nearby towns. A city should emphasize neighborhoods, food, museums, architecture, nightlife, shopping, cultural experiences, and day trips. A food-rich destination should expose food as its own planning surface, not bury it inside generic "things to do."

### Food And Drink Discovery

Food and drink should be a dedicated MLP category with its own sourcing, ranking, and card patterns.

TravelGPT should identify:

- Iconic dishes and drinks
- Food crawls and tasting routes
- Markets, food halls, bakeries, street-food areas, and culinary neighborhoods
- Farms, producers, distilleries, breweries, wineries, tea estates, coffee farms, spice markets, and other origin experiences
- Cooking classes, tastings, workshops, chef's tables, and hosted meals
- Vegetarian, vegan, halal, kosher, allergy-aware, and kid-friendly food options
- Regional specialties that differ from the broader country or state
- Food experiences that are worth scheduling around, not just nearby meal fillers

The food layer should answer both "where should I eat?" and "how do I understand this place through food?"

Examples:

- India: street-food crawls, regional thalis, biryani routes, tea or spice experiences, vegetarian-friendly local specialties
- Edinburgh: whisky tastings, distillery day trips, pub culture, Scottish food classics, food markets
- Kentucky: bourbon trail planning, distilleries, tastings, barbecue, regional food stops
- Big Island: coffee farms, farmers markets, poke, fruit stands, local bakeries, farm tours
- Paris: pastry routes, market streets, cheese and wine tastings, neighborhood bistros, food halls

Food recommendations should include dietary suitability and tradeoffs. If an experience is not vegetarian-friendly, not kid-friendly, alcohol-centered, expensive, reservation-heavy, or touristy-but-still-worth-it, the card should say so plainly.

### Local Intelligence And Experience Hacks

Every meaningful experience should include practical local intelligence when available.

TravelGPT should try to surface:

- Best time of day: sunrise, morning, afternoon, golden hour, sunset, night
- Times to avoid because of crowds, heat, traffic, parking, cruise arrivals, school groups, or tour buses
- Seasonal considerations: weather, closures, bloom periods, water conditions, snow, monsoon, fire season, festival calendars
- How long to budget so the user does not under-plan the experience
- What to bring: beach gear, layers, water shoes, picnic, cash, motion-sickness medicine, hiking shoes, swimsuit, towel, sunscreen, snacks, stroller, carrier
- How to experience it better: bike instead of drive, arrive by ferry, combine with a nearby market, reserve a timed slot, go after dinner, enter from a quieter side, visit during low tide
- Hidden friction: parking, permits, tickets, cell service, restroom access, shade, steep walks, limited food nearby, reservation scarcity
- Nearby pairings: food stops, viewpoints, short hikes, playgrounds, scenic routes, rainy-day backups

This is the product's "local friend" layer. It should help users avoid the painful moment of arriving somewhere and realizing they should have come at sunset, brought a volleyball, budgeted two more hours, booked ahead, rented bikes, packed swimsuits, or skipped the busiest window.

This layer should be sourced and scored separately from generic popularity. A famous experience with weak practical guidance may be less useful than a well-explained experience with clear timing and execution advice.

### Experience Cards

Each experience should be represented as a card with a compact preview and a deeper detail view.

The preview card should include:

- Experience name
- Category tags
- One-sentence summary
- Why it is worth considering
- Best for
- Approximate duration
- Effort level
- Booking difficulty
- Seasonality or timing note
- Local hack or "how to do it well" note
- Save / dismiss / more like this controls

The detail view should include:

- What the experience is
- Why it is special
- Who should do it
- Who should skip it
- Best time to go
- How to do it
- How to experience it well
- Common variants
- What to watch out for
- What to pack or prepare
- Local timing, crowd, parking, weather, or routing tips
- Nearby pairings or add-ons
- Accessibility or mobility notes when available
- Food, dietary, or kid-relevance when applicable
- Source links or booking/search links

### De-Duplication

TravelGPT should cluster duplicate or near-duplicate experiences into one canonical experience.

For example, a destination may have dozens of listings for the same underlying activity:

- Manta ray night snorkel
- Seine dinner cruise
- Louvre guided tour
- Haleakala sunrise tour
- Pasta-making class in Rome
- Champagne tasting day trip from Paris

The product should show the user the experience once, then explain variants and booking considerations inside the detail view.

This is a core differentiator. TravelGPT is not just another list of links. It is an experience-level understanding layer above search results and booking catalogs.

### Personalization

Personalization should affect ranking, filtering, and explanations.

Examples:

- Vegetarian traveler: surface vegetarian-friendly food experiences; flag meat-heavy experiences with explanation
- Food-driven traveler: elevate crawls, markets, tastings, food neighborhoods, producers, and iconic local dishes
- Family with young kids: prioritize lower-effort, stroller-friendly, flexible, or shorter activities
- Hiker: rank trails, viewpoints, nature reserves, scenic routes, and outdoor day trips higher
- Older parents: flag high-exertion or accessibility-sensitive experiences
- Rainy season: promote indoor, flexible, or weather-resilient options
- Special occasion: surface romantic, premium, private, scenic, or celebratory options

The product should avoid over-pruning. Users should be able to see stretch options and understand why something may or may not fit them.

### Shortlist

Users can save experiences into a shortlist.

The shortlist is the bridge between Phase 1 and Phase 2. It should help users form a point of view about the trip they want before asking for an itinerary.

The shortlist should support:

- Save
- Dismiss
- Mark as must-do
- Mark as maybe
- Add notes
- Share
- Export or copy links

## Phase 2: Itinerary Generation

The second phase turns a user's shortlist into a realistic itinerary.

This phase should not be required for the initial MLP, but the Phase 1 data model should be designed with it in mind.

The itinerary planner should account for:

- Trip dates
- Opening hours
- Geography and travel time
- Activity duration
- Meal breaks
- Energy and pace
- Weather sensitivity
- Reservation or ticket requirements
- Kid, dietary, mobility, and budget constraints
- Backup options

The output should include:

- Day-by-day schedule
- Map view
- Booking or source links
- "Swap this" controls
- "Make this day lighter" controls
- "Plan around rain" controls
- "We are running late" replanning

Phase 2 should still avoid end-to-end booking and payments. The initial itinerary product should help users decide what to book and when, then provide links and instructions.

## Non-Goals For The MLP

The first version will not:

- Book flights
- Book hotels
- Take payments
- Store passports or sensitive traveler documents
- Handle cancellations or refunds
- Guarantee availability
- Replace licensed travel agents for complex trips
- Promise that every "hidden gem" is unknown or uncrowded
- Generate a full itinerary before the user has explored and selected experiences

These constraints keep the product focused and reduce operational risk.

## Customer-Facing FAQ

### How is TravelGPT different from Google Search?

Google gives you links. TravelGPT gives you a structured understanding of a destination.

Instead of opening dozens of tabs and manually comparing blogs, maps, videos, and booking sites, you enter a destination and get a curated board of things worth considering. TravelGPT groups duplicate experiences, organizes recommendations by trip style, and explains why each option matters.

### How is TravelGPT different from TripAdvisor or booking sites?

Booking sites are optimized around listings and suppliers. That means the same underlying activity can appear dozens of times from different operators.

TravelGPT is organized around experiences. It shows you the activity first, explains whether it fits your trip, and then helps you understand the main ways to do it.

### Is this just another top-10 list?

No. TravelGPT includes famous must-sees when they matter, but it also surfaces local, niche, seasonal, and preference-specific experiences. The goal is not to give everyone the same checklist. The goal is to help each traveler understand the full menu of worthwhile possibilities.

### Does TravelGPT help with food?

Yes. Food and drink are first-class travel experiences in TravelGPT.

For many destinations, food is one of the main reasons to go. TravelGPT helps users discover iconic dishes, food crawls, markets, culinary neighborhoods, local producers, distilleries, wineries, breweries, farms, tastings, and cooking classes. It also considers dietary preferences and flags experiences that may not fit, such as alcohol-centered tours, meat-heavy food crawls, expensive tasting menus, or reservation-heavy restaurants.

The goal is not just to answer "where should I eat?" It is to help users understand the destination through food.

### Does TravelGPT include local hacks?

Yes. TravelGPT should explain how to experience a place well, not just name the place.

When available, recommendations should include the best time to go, what time to avoid, what to bring, how long to budget, whether parking or reservations are difficult, whether sunset or sunrise matters, what nearby add-on improves the stop, and what common mistake travelers make.

This is one of the main ways TravelGPT reduces FOMO. The user should not arrive somewhere and realize too late that they should have brought beach gear, booked earlier, rented bikes, planned for sunset, or saved more time.

### Can I use it if I already enjoy planning?

Yes. TravelGPT is not trying to take away the fun of planning. It removes the repetitive research work: duplicate listings, scattered recommendations, conflicting blogs, and endless tabs.

You still make the choices. TravelGPT helps you make them faster and with more confidence.

### Does it personalize recommendations?

Yes, but lightly at first. You can add preferences such as food interests, vegetarian needs, kids, mobility, budget, pace, adventure level, and trip occasion. TravelGPT uses those signals to rank, flag, and explain experiences.

It should not hide the destination from you. It should help you understand what fits and why.

### Will TravelGPT book things for me?

Not in the first version. The MLP focuses on discovery and shortlisting. Experience cards can include booking links, search links, and practical guidance, but users remain in control of booking.

### Will it create an itinerary?

Itinerary generation is Phase 2. The first version helps you discover and shortlist the experiences that matter. Once that works well, TravelGPT will turn your selected experiences into a realistic schedule.

### Why start with discovery instead of itinerary generation?

An itinerary is only as good as the experiences inside it. If the product starts by scheduling generic or poorly matched recommendations, the schedule may look polished but still feel wrong.

TravelGPT starts where trust begins: helping users understand what is worth doing.

## Internal FAQ

### What is the core job to be done?

When I am planning a trip, help me quickly understand the best and most relevant things I could do at a destination, so I can stop researching endlessly and confidently choose what belongs in my trip.

### What is the first lovable moment?

The user enters a destination, sees a clean board of well-organized experience cards, and thinks:

"This is exactly the overview I wish I had before opening 50 tabs."

The second lovable moment is seeing duplicate booking-site noise collapsed into a single understandable experience.

The third is adding personal preferences and watching the board become more relevant without becoming narrow or boring.

### What is the primary wedge?

Experience-level discovery, not booking and not generic itinerary generation.

TravelGPT should own the layer between raw search results and final trip planning: the point where a traveler is trying to understand what is worth doing.

### What must be true for this to work?

- The recommendations must feel meaningfully better than generic LLM output
- The product must de-duplicate repeated experiences well
- Food and drink discovery must be strong enough to stand as its own reason to use the product
- Local timing, packing, routing, and crowd-avoidance guidance must be specific enough to reduce FOMO
- The cards must explain tradeoffs clearly
- Source freshness and confidence must be visible enough to earn trust
- Personalization must improve ranking without over-filtering
- The UX must help users reach a stopping point, not create more research work

### How will we source recommendations?

- Human curation for pilot destinations

The system should extract candidate experiences, cluster duplicates, classify them, score them, and generate clear user-facing cards with source links.

Food and local-intelligence sourcing may require specialized workflows. The product should search not only for attractions and tours, but also for dish-level guides, neighborhood food guides, local restaurant roundups, distillery or winery routes, farmers markets, festival calendars, Reddit discussions, local blogs, timing advice, parking notes, seasonal warnings, and "what I wish I knew before visiting" content.

### Should this product exist, and should we build it?

Yes, but only if TravelGPT stays focused on destination discovery quality rather than becoming another generic AI itinerary generator.

The product should exist because travelers do not lack information. They lack synthesis, judgment, de-duplication, local nuance, and confidence that they understand a destination well enough to stop researching. The most valuable version of TravelGPT is not "AI plans your trip." It is "TravelGPT helps you understand what is actually worth doing here, why it matters, how to do it well, and what you would otherwise miss."

This is a meaningful problem. It is also a dangerous market.

Generic AI travel planning is already crowded. Large platforms such as Google, Airbnb, Booking, Expedia, and general-purpose AI assistants have structural advantages: maps, reviews, photos, flights, hotels, location data, booking integrations, and existing user intent. If TravelGPT tries to compete as a broad AI travel agent or generic itinerary generator, it is likely to be squeezed by larger platforms and become hard to differentiate.

TravelGPT should be built only around the sharper wedge:

- Experience-level de-duplication: show the underlying activity once, not dozens of supplier listings
- Food and drink as destination intelligence: help users understand a place through dishes, markets, crawls, producers, tastings, and rituals
- Local execution intelligence: best time to go, what to avoid, what to bring, how long to budget, how to pair nearby stops, and what common mistake to avoid
- FOMO reduction: help users feel they understand the destination well enough to make confident choices

The hard truth is that the product will fail if its cards feel like prettier ChatGPT output. The hard part is not generating text or building a board UI. The hard part is consistently producing better destination intelligence than a motivated traveler can get from Google, YouTube, Reddit, booking sites, and a general-purpose AI assistant.

We should build this only if we are willing to be obsessive about:

- Curation quality
- Source freshness
- De-duplication
- Food and drink depth
- Local timing and practical hacks
- Clear tradeoffs
- Trustworthy explanations
- High-quality pilot destinations before broad coverage

The first validation should be manual. Before building a large automated research system, we should create a few excellent destination boards by hand and test whether real travelers say:

- "This reduced my need to search elsewhere."
- "I discovered something I would not have found on my own."
- "I feel less afraid of missing out."
- "I would save or share this."
- "I would use this before Google or YouTube for my next trip."
- "I would pay for this before an important trip."

If users do not love the manually curated version, automation will not save the product. If they do love it, automation should be used to scale the research, clustering, scoring, and card generation workflows without losing the taste and judgment that made the manual version work.

The strategic rule is:

**Build TravelGPT as the best destination intelligence board on the internet. Do not build it as another AI trip planner.**

### What are the biggest risks?

**Generic output risk:** The product may feel like a prettier ChatGPT response unless sourcing, de-duplication, and card quality are strong.

**Freshness risk:** Travel recommendations go stale. Closures, seasonal changes, construction, and policy changes can break trust.

**Local nuance risk:** Weak or generic "tips" can damage trust. Local intelligence must be specific, practical, and sourced or clearly reasoned.

**Food quality risk:** Food recommendations are highly personal and can become generic fast. The product must distinguish iconic dishes, true local institutions, tourist-friendly classics, dietary fit, and overrated hype.

**Coverage risk:** The product may work well for famous destinations but poorly for long-tail locations.

**Trust risk:** Users need to know why something is recommended and where the information came from.

**Over-personalization risk:** If filtering is too aggressive, users may miss the joy of discovery.

**UX sprawl risk:** The board can become overwhelming if categories and cards are not carefully designed.

### How will we measure success?

Early success should be measured by whether users feel they can stop researching.

Potential metrics:

- Destination search to saved experience conversion
- Number of experiences saved per session
- Dismiss / save / "more like this" interaction rate
- Share rate of destination boards or shortlists
- Return rate for the same trip
- Qualitative answer to: "Did this reduce your need to search elsewhere?"
- Qualitative answer to: "Did you discover something you would not have found on your own?"
- Qualitative answer to: "Did this help you understand the food or drink scene?"
- Qualitative answer to: "Did the local tips prevent a mistake or improve how you planned the experience?"
- Number of saved food and drink experiences per destination search
- Engagement with local tips, packing notes, timing notes, and nearby pairings
- Willingness to recommend
- Willingness to use TravelGPT before Google or YouTube for the next trip

### What should we manually validate first?

Before building a large automated system, we should manually create high-quality boards for a small number of destinations and test whether users love the format.

Good pilot destinations:

- Big Island, Hawaii
- Maui
- Seattle
- Paris
- Lake Tahoe
- New York City
- Kyoto

Manual curation will teach us what the automated research pipeline needs to produce.

## Launch Scope

The first launch should support a small number of high-quality destinations rather than broad, thin coverage.

Recommended alpha:

- 3-5 destinations
- 75-150 canonical experiences per destination
- 10-15 adaptive categories per destination
- Dedicated food and drink category per destination where relevant
- Local tips, timing, packing, and pairing notes on high-value cards
- Preference-based ranking
- Save / dismiss / shortlist
- Experience detail pages
- Source and booking links
- Shareable shortlist

This is enough to test the core claim: TravelGPT helps travelers understand a destination faster and better than their current research process.

## Product Principles

### Experience-first, not listing-first

Show the traveler the thing worth doing before showing them every provider who sells it.

### Curated, not infinite

The product should reduce cognitive load. More results are not always better.

### Explain the why

Every recommendation should make its reasoning legible.

### Food is travel

Treat food, drink, markets, producers, and culinary rituals as core destination experiences, not as a restaurant sidebar.

### Teach users how to experience the place

The product should surface local timing, packing, routing, crowd, parking, weather, and pairing advice that makes an experience meaningfully better.

### Preserve discovery

Personalization should guide users, not trap them in a narrow profile.

### Be honest about confidence

If something is seasonal, hard to book, physically demanding, expensive, or uncertain, say so.

### Build toward itinerary, but do not rush it

The discovery layer should produce structured data that makes scheduling possible later.

TravelGPT starts as the best way to discover and shortlist worthwhile travel experiences for a destination, then grows into the itinerary planner once users trust the things it recommends.
